\title[DeepDive for Active Literature Exploration]{DeepDive: Supporting Active Literature Exploration with an AI-Structured Interface}

\author{Laieh Jwella}

\begin{abstract}
AI tools can make early-stage literature review feel fast and productive by returning paper lists, summaries, and recommendations on demand. However, quick retrieval does not necessarily support active understanding of a research space, and may encourage users to accept papers as relevant without being able to explain how those papers relate to one another or to their own research direction. This paper presents \textit{DeepDive}, an AI-supported interface for early-stage literature exploration that restructures literature search as a guided workflow rather than an immediate retrieval step. To evaluate this interaction model, DeepDive was compared with a constrained flat-list baseline in a small counterbalanced within-subjects study with five master's-level students. In this sample, participants reported higher perceived understanding, engagement, and ability to articulate why selected papers mattered when using DeepDive than when using the baseline. The strongest qualitative patterns centered on contribution-framed cards and research-area structure, while critiques emerged around priming, ontology trust, and workflow friction. The findings suggest that structuring literature exploration may support more intentional paper selection and stronger articulation during early-stage review, while also highlighting the need for contestable system-authored structure.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{AI-supported learning, literature exploration, information seeking, human-computer interaction, scaffolding}

\section{Introduction}
AI-assisted literature search has become a common starting point for students beginning a research project. With a short prompt, a system can return paper lists, summaries, and recommendations almost instantly. This can make literature review feel efficient, but efficiency is not the same as understanding. A student may leave with several apparently relevant papers and still be unable to explain what conversation those papers belong to, what tensions exist between them, or why one paper should be preferred over another.

This concern motivated \textit{DeepDive}. The problem was not framed as whether AI can find literature, because in practice it already can. The more important question was whether the interaction model around retrieval helps users make sense of what has been found. If an interface collapses the task into a single prompt-to-list step, then the user may be nudged toward selection without synthesis. The result is a reading list that looks productive on the surface but is weakly understood by the person who assembled it.

This concern aligns with prior work suggesting that AI-generated outputs can feel easier and faster while still producing shallower understanding than more active search practices~\cite{melumad2025llmlearning}. It also aligns with the view that searching is itself a learning process rather than only a retrieval task~\cite{vakkari2016searching,camara2021searching}. Work on exploratory search interfaces further suggests that interface structure can shape how users navigate ambiguity and construct understanding, rather than merely how quickly they retrieve results~\cite{white2005exploratory}. For literature recommendation systems specifically, explanations and user control matter because users need to understand why items are being recommended, not simply accept that they are relevant~\cite{guesmi2024interactive}. Together, these perspectives suggest that the interface around retrieval may matter as much as retrieval quality itself.

To investigate that issue, this paper presents \textit{DeepDive}, an interface that asks users to clarify concerns, confirm conceptual research areas, compare papers through contribution-framed cards, and assemble a literature foundation before citation export is made available.

\paragraph{Research Question.} \textit{How does a structured AI-supported literature exploration workflow influence users' confidence in their literature selections and their ability to articulate why selected papers are relevant, compared with a flat-list retrieval interface?}

The contribution is twofold. First, the paper presents a design contribution in the form of a structured interaction model for literature exploration. Second, it reports an exploratory evaluation comparing DeepDive with a constrained flat-list baseline. The evaluation focuses on practically relevant outcomes: perceived understanding, engagement, confidence, and the ability to articulate why selected papers belong in an initial reading list. The findings are presented as directional patterns from a small course-project study rather than as population-level effects.

\section{Related Work}
\subsection{AI assistance, speed, and shallow understanding}
Recent discussion around AI-supported learning has emphasized a tension between speed and depth. Melumad and Yun argue that LLM-generated outputs can make learning feel easier while producing shallower understanding than web-search-based learning activities~\cite{melumad2025llmlearning}. For literature review, that distinction matters because the task is not only to obtain sources. Students also need to judge what each source contributes, how sources cluster into areas, and which tensions or gaps those sources reveal.

\subsection{Searching as part of learning}
Vakkari's account of searching as learning reframes information seeking as a process through which understanding develops~\cite{vakkari2016searching}. Related work on searching to learn with instructional scaffolding similarly suggests that search environments can be designed to actively support sense-making rather than simply return results~\cite{camara2021searching}. From that perspective, the interface is not just a container for results. It becomes part of the learning environment itself, shaping what the user notices, compares, and carries forward as a representation of the field.

\subsection{Explanations and control in literature recommendation}
Work on exploratory search interfaces argues that interface design matters when users are still trying to understand a topic rather than execute a fixed query~\cite{white2005exploratory}. In parallel, work on explainable literature recommendation highlights the need for user control and explanations around suggested sources~\cite{guesmi2024interactive}. In such systems, relevance alone is insufficient. Recommendations need to be legible enough for users to understand why they appeared and whether they belong in the current task. DeepDive extends this logic from paper-level recommendation toward the level of conceptual structure itself.

\section{DeepDive System}
DeepDive was designed around three goals: helping users clarify a still-forming topic, making the conceptual shape of the literature visible, and supporting intentional paper selection through better articulation of why selected papers belong together. A central design principle is \textit{understanding before extraction}. Rather than making citation export immediately available, the system delays export until after users have shaped the topic, explored research areas, and assembled a literature foundation.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{assets/fig-workflow}
  \caption{\figcap{Workflow overview of DeepDive. The system restructures early-stage literature review into a sequence of reflective steps rather than an immediate retrieval interaction.}}
  \Description{Workflow overview image for the DeepDive process.}
  \label{fig:workflow}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:workflow} shows the overall workflow. Users begin with a rough topic, respond to topic-mapping prompts, confirm proposed research areas, compare papers within those areas, and finally assemble a literature foundation. The importance of this sequence is not simply that it is longer than a conventional chat interaction. Each step surfaces a different kind of judgment: what matters in the topic, how the field might be structured, which papers serve different roles, and whether the emerging reading list has a shape the user can actually explain.

\subsection{Topic Mapping as concern-first entry}
After topic entry, the system asks exploratory questions intended to make the user's concerns explicit before papers appear. This is one of the most distinctive parts of the interface. Instead of treating the user's initial prompt as complete, DeepDive asks what aspects of the topic feel most important, uncertain, or worrying. The intention is twofold: to help the user clarify their own priorities, and to give the system a more interpretable basis for structuring what comes next.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.88\linewidth]{assets/fig2-topic-mapping}
  \caption{\figcap{Topic Mapping asks users to articulate concerns before literature is shown, making early assumptions explicit rather than letting the first retrieved list define the field.}}
  \Description{The Topic Mapping screen of DeepDive.}
  \label{fig:topicmapping}
\end{figure}

This step is meant to reinforce active involvement at the very start of the workflow. Rather than asking users to react to whatever list appears first, it encourages them to state a perspective from which the literature should be explored. At the same time, the evaluation later showed that this step can introduce friction for users whose topic already feels clear, making it both a strength and a site of tension in the design.

\subsection{Research Area Discovery as contestable structure}
After Topic Mapping, the system proposes conceptual research areas through a confirmation step rather than silently imposing them. This makes the system-authored structure visible and contestable.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.88\linewidth]{assets/fig3-area-confirmation}
  \caption{\figcap{Research Area Discovery makes system-authored structure visible and contestable through explicit user confirmation.}}
  \Description{The Research Area Discovery modal in DeepDive.}
  \label{fig:areas}
\end{figure}

This design choice matters because it preserves user agency at the point where the system begins to shape the conceptual map of the field. The interface does not simply reveal tabs or clusters as if they were unquestionably correct. Instead, it asks for assent. In design terms, this is meant to shift the interface from opaque recommendation toward guided negotiation of structure.

\subsection{Contribution-framed comparison in Literature Exploration}
Once areas are confirmed, papers appear within those areas. A key design decision is that cards foreground contribution rather than title. This is meant to support comparison at the level of what a paper offers rather than only whether its title looks relevant.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.88\linewidth]{assets/fig4-literature-exploration}
  \caption{\figcap{DeepDive's Literature Exploration view organizes papers by confirmed areas and frames them by contribution rather than title alone.}}
  \Description{The Literature Exploration view of DeepDive.}
  \label{fig:exploration}
\end{figure}

This view is where the workflow turns from structure-building into selection. By grouping papers inside confirmed areas and giving each one a contribution-oriented framing, the interface tries to encourage intentional selection rather than title recognition or rank-following. Users are meant to compare how papers differ, complement one another, or occupy different roles within the space.

\subsection{Literature Foundation as understanding-before-export}
The final Literature Foundation screen groups selected papers by area and only then exposes citation export. This reframes the output of the interaction as a structured reading foundation rather than a flat list of saved citations.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.88\linewidth]{assets/fig5-foundation}
  \caption{\figcap{The Literature Foundation screen assembles a structured reading list and places citation export after exploration rather than before it.}}
  \Description{The Literature Foundation view of DeepDive.}
  \label{fig:foundation}
\end{figure}

That ordering is deliberate. The system is designed so that export follows topic shaping, area confirmation, and comparison, rather than short-circuiting them. In other words, the final screen operationalizes the broader principle of understanding before extraction.

\section{Method}
\subsection{Study design}
The evaluation asked whether a structured exploration workflow would affect participants' confidence in their literature selections and their ability to articulate why those selections mattered, compared to an unstructured AI-retrieval baseline. DeepDive was compared with a constrained flat-list baseline in a within-subjects, counterbalanced design. Each participant completed both conditions, with condition order crossed against topic order so that any order effect was not confounded with a single topic.

The baseline was intentionally constrained in order to compare two interaction models on more balanced terms: immediate retrieval versus structured exploration. In the baseline condition, participants received a flat list of papers with titles and short excerpts, without topic mapping, research-area confirmation, or contribution-framed organization. In the DeepDive condition, participants moved through the staged workflow described above. Because both interfaces were prototypes, the baseline did not fully reproduce the responsiveness participants might associate with a production chatbot. This is both a limitation of the implementation and a consequence of the study's scope-matching design choice.

\subsection{Participants, task, and procedure}
Five master's-level students known to the researcher volunteered to participate. All were enrolled in master's-level courses and had prior exposure to academic literature. Participants completed the same high-level task in both conditions: select three to five papers for an initial reading list on an assigned topic.

Two topics of comparable breadth and difficulty were used: AI's impact on student learning behaviour and AI's impact on academic literature discovery. The same underlying paper pool was reused across conditions, and each participant saw one topic per condition rather than the same topic twice. Per-session duration was approximately 50--60 minutes, including introduction and consent, two task conditions with think-aloud, post-condition Likert ratings, comparative questions at the end, and a short debrief.

\subsection{Measures and analysis}
After each condition, participants rated confidence, articulation, perceived understanding, and active engagement on a five-point Likert scale. They also completed an open-ended articulation prompt asking them to describe the research area, explain how the selected papers related to it, and justify why those papers had been chosen. Think-aloud observations were captured through session notes and supported by recordings and transcription.

Quantitative analysis remained descriptive: per-item means and condition differences were summarized without statistical inference. Qualitative material was coded thematically over multiple passes by the researcher using three dimensions defined in the evaluation protocol: structural understanding, comparison-based reasoning, and intentionality cues. The purpose of the qualitative analysis was not to build an exhaustive coding system, but to test whether the kind of articulation encouraged by DeepDive differed meaningfully from the articulation produced under the baseline.

\section{Results}
\subsection{Quantitative pattern}
Across this sample, DeepDive produced higher mean ratings on all four measured items than the constrained baseline. The strongest shifts were in active engagement, perceived understanding, and articulation, while confidence increased more modestly.

The largest shift appears on active engagement (+2.4), followed by perceived understanding (+2.2) and articulation (+2.0). Confidence also increased, but by a smaller margin (+1.0). Given the small sample size, these results should be interpreted as exploratory indications rather than evidence of generalizable effects. They are best read as a consistent directional pattern within this sample, not as proof of a stable population-level advantage.

All five participants reported that DeepDive helped them understand the topic better, and all five said they would trust it more as a starting point for a real assignment, although those trust judgments came with caveats. This makes the pattern hard to ignore within the study, but the same unanimity is also one reason the results must be framed cautiously.

\subsection{Qualitative pattern}
The clearest qualitative shift appeared in participants' open-ended explanations. Under the baseline, several responses stopped at relevance: papers matched the topic, seemed appropriate, or looked like reasonable starting points. Under DeepDive, participants more often described sub-areas, tensions between papers, and the specific role individual papers played in the broader topic. The explanations therefore became more structural and comparative.

The findings notes coded articulation along three dimensions: structural understanding, comparison-based reasoning, and intentionality cues. Under the baseline, three of five participants were rated absent on both structural understanding and comparison-based reasoning. Under DeepDive, all five participants reached the strongest rating on all three dimensions. This does not establish a general effect, but it does make the articulation shift one of the clearest qualitative signals in the present study.

Two interface elements carried most of the qualitative weight. First, contribution-framed cards helped participants compare papers on the basis of what each paper offered rather than what it was called. Second, the area-discovery step gave participants a vocabulary for discussing the shape of the research space. In the findings notes, one participant described the area modal as the moment ``where it clicked,'' while another emphasized that the structure helped turn papers into positions in a conversation rather than a pile of related items.

\subsection{Critiques and caveats}
The evaluation also surfaced a coherent set of critiques. Participants noted that topic mapping could do more priming work than the design explicitly acknowledged, especially when the offered options constrained how a topic was framed. Others suggested that system-authored areas risk becoming a \textit{soft cage} unless users can add or reshape the conceptual structure themselves. A related critique concerned trust relocation: the design may reduce over-trust in paper lists while increasing trust in the system's ontology of the field. Finally, participants who already felt clear about their topic found the survey step more frictional, and the chat sidebar was sometimes perceived as secondary.

\section{Discussion}
The results suggest that the main value of DeepDive lies in helping users move from selecting papers that look relevant to articulating why specific papers belong together in an early-stage reading list. This is the strongest claim supported by the data. The study does \emph{not} show that DeepDive produces objectively better literature reviews, objectively better paper choices, or durable learning gains. What it does show is that, in this sample, the structured interaction model changed how participants described the topic and justified their selections.

One useful interpretation is an articulation--confidence gap. In the baseline condition, participants could often feel that their choices were acceptable while still producing thin explanations. In the DeepDive condition, confidence also increased, but the more meaningful change was in the quality of justification. This is why the open-ended articulation prompt mattered so much in the study: it tested not only whether users felt good about the output, but whether they could explain the structure behind it.

The study also clarifies an important design tension. Structure can scaffold understanding, but it can also constrain it. Several participant critiques point toward the same lesson: system-authored structure should remain contestable. In practical terms, that means future versions should allow users to reject, extend, and revise the area decomposition rather than merely accept it.

\section{Limitations and Conclusion}
This study has several important limitations. It involved a small convenience sample of five participants, relied heavily on subjective measures and self-report, and took place in a single session. The baseline condition was intentionally constrained and therefore does not represent the full capability of a production conversational AI system. Multiple design features were bundled together in the DeepDive condition, so the study cannot isolate whether the observed differences were driven primarily by area structure, contribution framing, or another component. The study also did not test failure cases in which the system's proposed ontology is poor or misleading.

Within those limits, the findings still support a useful conclusion for the scope of this project. DeepDive suggests that an AI-supported interface for literature exploration can be designed around active understanding rather than immediate extraction. In this sample, participants using DeepDive reported stronger perceived understanding, higher engagement, and better ability to articulate their paper choices than when using a constrained flat-list baseline. Just as importantly, the evaluation surfaced concrete tensions around priming, contestability, and trust that should shape future iterations of structured literature exploration systems.
